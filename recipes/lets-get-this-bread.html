“Let's Get This” Rye Bread

Description

Rye bread is so named because of the distinctive tangy flavor it gets from caraway seeds. I know this doesn't make perfect sense, but those are the rules; I don't make them. Coincidentally this flavor note is balanced by the earthiness of rye flour, but don't make the same mistake I have in the past. Literal “rye” bread without wheat flower is more of a rye biscuit or hardtack.

This bread; however, aspires to a “let's get this” vigor. For that purpose a sourdough would be ideal, but I have to admit I baked the entire sourdough colony last time and the new one is still a tad uncouth. As is typical with homemade bread recipes, the quantities are approximate and should be adjusted by feel. Go forth and get the bread that is yours.

Ingredients

  • active dry yeast
  • water
  • 100g rye flour
  • 600g bread flour
  • light molasses
  • 20g of salt
  • 30g of caraway seeds

Steps

  1. Pre-mix the flour blend. You don't have to do this; you can also eyeball the amount of rye to wheat.
  2. Let's start this bread with a pouliche, an overnight pre-ferment. Start with about 200mL of water, little bit of molasses a pinch of yeast, and gently stir in enough flour to make a pudding texture. Cover with a moist towel and let it go 12-24 hours.
  3. Dissolve salt into another 200-300mL of water and stir in to loosen the dough to its most liquid state.
  4. Stir in caraway seeds.
  5. Stir in flour. How much? Enough to make a sticky dough.
  6. Let rise for about an hour.
  7. Now let's get this kneaded, gently!
    • Dust the surface with flour, enough to make it not quite so sticky. Peel it up from the bowl and get the bottom side too.
    • Stretch it but don't tear, then fold across the direction you stretch.
    • Turn, do the other direction.
    • Get three or four stretch-fold cycles in
    • Flip the dough over so that the folds sit on the bottom and a nice smooth surface on top.
  8. Allow another half hour of rising time and repeat until you've done a total of three or four kneading cycles.
  9. Preheat oven to 250C with the lid of a dutch oven inside. You can preheat the dutch oven for better results, but you'll have to be careful when you put the loaf in.
  10. But I'm a bit more cautious and prefer to let the loaf rise for the final time inside a cool dutch oven.
  11. Score the top of the loaf with a razor.
  12. Bake, covered, for 30 minutes.
  13. Remove cover and bake for about another 15 minutes, to an internal temperature of 90C.
  14. Thoroughly cool.
